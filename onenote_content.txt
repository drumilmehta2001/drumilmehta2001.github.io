People buy options on futures contracts instead of stocks to gain massive leverage, avoid strict margin requirements, and trade in markets that are virtually impossible to access via standard equities.&nbsp;
1. Superior Leverage and Capital Efficiency
Options on futures offer extreme leverage. A relatively small premium allows an investor to control a massive underlying notional value. For example, a single option contract on the E-mini S&amp;P 500 (/ES) controls exposure to 50 times the S&amp;P 500 index value.&nbsp;
2. Built-In Limited Risk
While futures contracts carry theoretically infinite downside (requiring you to post more money if the trade goes against you, known as margin calls), buying an option on a future caps your absolute maximum loss at the premium (the price you paid for the option).&nbsp;
3. Avoiding Strict Margin Calls
Futures themselves are &quot;marked to market&quot; daily, requiring traders to maintain specific cash balances in their accounts at the end of every trading day. If the market drops, the broker requires more capital instantly. Buying options eliminates this overnight mark-to-market stress because your position is fully paid for upfront.&nbsp;
4. Access to Commodities and Hard Assets
You cannot directly buy or short stocks of certain commodities like crude oil, gold, or wheat. Options on futures provide a vehicle to speculate on or hedge against the price swings of physical raw materials, interest rates, and global currencies.&nbsp;
5. Round-the-Clock Flexibility
Futures markets—and by extension, options on many futures—trade almost 24/5. This allows investors to react to global news, earnings reports, or geopolitical events outside of regular U.S. stock market hours (9:30 AM to 4:00 PM EST).&nbsp;
The further out an options contract is, the more expensive the premium, because there’s more likelihood the option will expire ITM. So as time goes on, and the expiry date gets closer, the value of the option will decrease. Thus, Theta is negative because the option gets cheaper as time goes forward.

The &quot;Grid Trading&quot; Strategy (Statistical Market Making)
This is the retail version of Avellaneda-Stoikov. It works best in &quot;Sideways&quot; or &quot;Ranging&quot; markets.
The Logic: You place a &quot;grid&quot; of limit orders above and below the current price.
The Execution: * As price dips, it hits your Buy Limits.
As it bounces, it hits your Sell Limits.
You are essentially &quot;harvesting&quot; the noise of the market.
Why no colocation? Because you aren't trying to &quot;beat&quot; someone to a price. Your orders are already sitting there (resting) on the book. You are waiting for the market to come to you.
If your bot is making a market and a CHoCH happens, the price will trend hard in one direction. Your bot will get &quot;Steamrolled&quot; (it will keep buying as the price crashes).
The Fix: Program your bot to Shut Down if it detects a CHoCH on a higher timeframe.
